# Meditation app design and spiritual practice philosophy with Hitesh
## Summary
### Meditation App: Concept and Demo
- Core idea: structured, programmable meditation sessions with variables, loops, and day-by-day progression
  - Teachers build scripts with configurable rounds, breaths, and pauses
  - Sessions arranged into multi-week/month programs
  - AI voices for now; plan to replace with teacher-recorded audio once scripts are finalized
- Personal trainer model applied to meditation teaching
  - Teachers assign programs to students, track practice history, check in weekly
  - Inspired by wanting a teacher-designed program without needing to be physically present
- Current name “Progress Meditation” acknowledged as weak; “Meditation Pro” floated as better
### Go-to-Market Thinking
- Proof-of-concept phase: find a few early users (body psychotherapist friend, Hitesh)
- Commercial paths being explored:
  - Teacher-branded programs (e.g. “Yoga with Hitesh”) with optional coaching tier
  - White-label deal with Resilient Minds: they embed the software in their app for first-responder mental health training
### Hitesh’s Feedback on the App
- Open to using it personally; wants an account to explore and build programs
- Key observation: younger users more likely to rely on technology and benefit from pre-loaded structure
  - Short attention spans and difficulty self-organizing make “click play and go” compelling
- Gamification concern raised by Miles; Hitesh reframed it as motivational psychology, not a spiritual problem
  - Gym analogy: knowing your reps and weight targets keeps you showing up
### Spiritual Practice: Structure vs. Presence
- Hitesh’s core challenge to Miles: goal-oriented practice risks always living in an imagined future
  - Every present-moment practice only valued for what it promises later
  - Spirituality differs from material goals: clarity is always available now, not earned over time
- Miles’s counter: developing the skill to perceive clearly is still a progressive process
  - Less social media, more time with friends, more present-moment awareness: all measurable qualitatively
  - Intuitive sense of “lighter sunglasses” days as the real metric
- Hitesh’s synthesis: regular practice is genuinely important, but simplicity beats complexity
  - The quiet moments in meditation are what was always being sought; any practice can reach them
  - Risk of accumulating practices: eventually forced back to the same stillness that was there on day one
- Hitesh’s personal experience: now just sits and “is” in meditation; the experience-hunting phase was real and worthwhile, but passed
  - His Facebook bio used to read “the experience hunter”
### Presence, Time, and Everyday Practice
- Hitesh on dilating time: doing one thing fully stretches the moment and creates effortless focus
  - Opposite of compressing time via alcohol or social media (Miles’s observation, Hitesh agreed)
  - Practical entry points: first few bites of a meal, making the bed, doing dishes with full attention
- Caution against using meditation to avoid problems: finishing a session and finding the problem still there
- Rupert Spira anecdote: longtime student felt peak experiences fading; Spira reframed it as the new normal
### Book and Mutual Collaboration
- Hitesh’s book sent to publishers last month; one rejection already, expects more
  - Plan B: self-publish in autumn
  - Needs help with Amazon promotion, format decisions, and distribution strategy
- Miles offered marketing support; agreed to scope it properly when the time comes
- Mutual exchange proposed:
  - Hitesh sends one chapter at a time with specific feedback questions
  - Miles shares the meditation app/website for Hitesh to review as a teacher
  - Reconvene after both have reviewed each other’s work
### Next Steps

- Read Hitesh’s book chapters as they arrive and give feedback (Miles)
