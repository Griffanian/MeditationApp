# Therapy session — urges, shame cycles, and mindfulness techniques with mentor
## Summary
### Urges This Week
- One evening episode: pornography blocked, ended in masturbation; felt manageable
- Two distinct urge contexts:
  - Out in public, mind drawn to people’s bodies
  - At home, sexual desire arising spontaneously
### Shame Cycles and Self-Judgment
- Key insight: unconscious glance at someone is no different neurologically than noticing a loud noise
  - Both are stimulus-response; the difference is how long attention stays there
  - Beating himself up over “moral failures” vs. practical ones is an inconsistent standard
- Secondary shame cycle identified as actively unhelpful
  - Analyzing and self-criticizing intensifies the emotion rather than resolving it
  - Keeps the urge alive by holding it in tension
- Therapist reframe: acceptance doesn’t mean permission or agreement
  - It means acknowledging “this is where I am now” without feeding the loop
- Fear of acceptance: worry that self-compassion will remove the drive to change
  - Rooted in years of self-discipline being the primary tool for self-improvement
### Public Urges and Body Tension
- Pattern when walking: notice urge, try to redirect, get pulled back, frustration builds
  - Frustration creates physical tension, which holds the urge in place and intensifies it
- Worst-case fear: grabbing someone (linked to past drunk incidents, not sober behavior)
  - Sober probability: zero, but the felt sense of certainty isn’t there
  - Fear is rooted in past experience, not present reality
- Therapist suggestion: check body posture when urge arises
  - Tension around jaw, shoulders feeds the urge; loosening the body removes the container
- Social norms question raised: when is it acceptable to look, for how long, in what way
  - Current internal rule is too absolutist (“never look with desire”)
  - Glancing is normal; the thought attached to the glance is what escalates it
### Mindfulness Technique: Observing and Releasing
- Distinction between three responses to an unwanted thought:
  - Pushing away: avoidance, boomerang effect, thought returns harder
  - Holding it: thought grows, feeds desire, feels like an infection
  - Observing and releasing: acknowledge, watch it pass, don’t attach
- Technique: name it internally (“I’m noticing an urge, I’m noticing tension”)
  - Second you observe it, the mind and body stop working to get your attention
  - Visualize it moving through: leaf on a stream, cloud in the sky, conveyor belt
- Key distinction: the thought moves away on its own; you don’t push it
- Expect it to take practice; the muscle needs repeated training before it holds
### Next Steps
- **Practice the observe-and-release technique daily** (Miles)

  Experiment with a visualization that fits (stream, conveyor belt, characters) and report back next session on what worked.
