# Therapy session — body acceptance, mindfulness practice, and self-compassion with therapist
## Summary
### Mindful Masturbation: Exploring a New Framework
- Came across an article on mindful masturbation as an approach to sexuality without pornography
- Reframing masturbation as healthy self-expression, separate from sex with a partner
- Appealing because it treats sexuality as a natural part of life rather than something to manage or suppress
  - Parallels to conscious eating: control means making deliberate choices, not abstinence
- Concern: the approach feels unfamiliar and requires slow, deliberate introduction
  - Slipped up before leaving York, visited an escort
  - Needs a better laptop blocking system; current one insufficient
### Body Image and Dissociation
- Long-standing discomfort with body: felt overweight since childhood, peaked at 150kg
  - Past self-criticism felt partially justified as a health warning; now more aesthetic and athletic goals
- Pattern in sexual experiences: used external focus (partner, pornography, fantasy) to escape body awareness
  - Goal-oriented rather than experience-oriented: needed to “finish” to feel validated
  - Underlying belief: “I don’t deserve this, I need to prove myself”
- Connecting to the body without distraction brings up discomfort, shame, and physical sensations
  - Pressure, a sense of “wrongness,” itchiness around areas like chest and stomach
  - Historically coped by pushing through (e.g., refusing to hide in changing rooms) rather than accepting
### Observer vs. Being: The Core Distinction
- Can observe internal sensations without difficulty, e.g., 10+ minutes of stillness in daily practice
- The harder thing: actually *being* in the body, not just watching it
  - Ordinary moments (walking home, eating) feel disconnected and require external stimulation
  - Spiritual “witness awareness” framing feels like another form of dissociation
- Fear that acceptance means stagnation: “if I stop judging, nothing will change”
  - Therapist challenged this: pain and judgment perpetuate the same cycle
- Inner dynamic identified: vulnerable part (“this is unfair”) quickly overridden by a protector/critic (“yeah, but get on with it”)
  - Linked to autism-related strong sense of injustice, and a learned counter-mechanism developed in childhood
  - Therapist noted this pattern invalidates the vulnerable part; suggested acknowledging it: “I hear you, it feels unfair”
### Practice Plan and Next Steps
- Start extremely small: spend a few minutes daily observing one low-stakes body feature
  - Starting point: hair on fingers (mildly odd, not emotionally loaded)
  - Use all five senses; label judgments as they arise rather than attaching to them (“I notice a judgment”)
  - Try both: notice the judgment, then restate factually (“that crease bends to the left”)
- Therapist suggested non-sexual self-care as a bridge: shower, cooking, brushing teeth, framed as acts of self-value
- Longer term: move from hands toward more charged areas of the body gradually
