# Gaia Sthiram meditation practice — body stillness and sensory awareness
### Summary
### What Is Gaia Sthiram

- Sanskrit: “Gaya” (body) + “Sthiram” (stillness)
- Foundational meditation practice to train the body to remain still
- Enables longer, deeper meditation sessions (yogis sit still for hours, days, or weeks)
- Entry point to advanced Himalayan meditation practices

### Why It Matters for Sensory Training

- Branches of Pratyahara involve training different senses
- Gaia Sthiram is especially suited to those anchored in proprioception or touch
- Stillness amplifies sensory awareness: simple sensations become vivid and intense

### The Core Insight

- Sitting still for a few minutes is one of the most intense experiences possible
- The breath becomes a profound relief: cool, refreshing, a “bastion of peace”
- Demonstrates that bliss is intrinsic to experience, not dependent on external achievement
- Attention is the variable: a mundane breath vs. a breath full of life differ only in awareness

### The Practice: Instructions

- Sit upright, spine and head straight; shoulders relaxed
- Close eyes and settle into the body before beginning
- Allow one final movement, then enter complete stillness
- No movement for the duration, including itches, twitches, or shifts
- Breath is the one permitted movement; use it as a refuge, then return to stillness
- Notice body signals without giving in to them

### Practice Structure (Two Versions Recorded)

- Long version: approximately 5 to 7 minutes
- Short version: approximately 2 minutes, with a countdown from 10 to 1 before release
- Closing: notice how alive and relieved the body feels upon moving again

### Philosophical Context

- Yogis in the Himalayas are not bored; they practice a far more advanced version of this
- Buddha’s focus on the breath in Vipassana and Buddhism traces to this same discovery
- Root cause of suffering: loss of control over attention
- Six minutes of stillness can be more intense than anything else done in a full day
